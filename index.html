Interest in IT

1. What is your interest in IT?

Information Technology is the means by which most of my interests are a product of, or gives tremendous assistance to. Games, videos, learning and communication are a few categorically broad interests I have that are impacted by it. What primarily influenced me was the growing desire to understand, replicate and draw influence from how IT was used to create and improve many of my interests. I reached an intense level of interest in IT around age 17. Things like video games became an obsession at that age. I believe I have an above-average understanding of how to use most IT programs that the general public would use. I have no knowledge worth noting past that level. I have no past participation in IT studies run by education providers. Most that I know about I.T. has come from using computer programs as an end-user.

2. Why did you choose to come to RMIT?

I chose to study this subject to put myself on a path towards being in a career I would be comfortable and productive in. When I realised that my current job is not where I want to be in the long-term, and that my current education would make it challenging to do anything about it, I decided to study something that would result in a career more suitable for me. Secondly, online study was the only option I had available for this course because of my current education. Even if I had the option, I would likely still choose online because the convenience and flexibility it brings are incredibly helpful. As for why I chose RMIT specifically, I knew it as a reputable University that people I know have said is a great choice for this field of study. So when I was offered enrolment after applying, it was an easy offer to accept.

3. What do you expect to learn during your studies?

I expect to learn fundamental I.T.-related skills that directly relate to the outlined topics within the Bachelor of I.T. During this course I hope to also learn other useful skills that are not specific to I.T. Universal skills such as time management, organisation and planning could improve by structuring days efficiently around study and meeting deadlines (without leaving things to the last minute). Work-coordination, assertiveness, communication and other skills could also see improvement from participating in group tasks. Going into this course with the little research I’ve done into the different fields of I.T. Cyber security and software development stand out the most as a future career. From what I can tell, both fields focus on logic, as well as often a strong reliance on fixed procedures. Following systems and logical thought are both attributes I excel at. Nearing the end of these studies, I expect to have made up my mind on which specific area of the IT field I want to pursue a career in.

Understanding My Personal Profile 

What do the results of these tests mean for you?

The combined result of these three tests I took summarise many of my personal traits. Below are some highlights of major aspects of the results.

Out of my top three personality type matches my Myers-Briggs Type Indicator test concluded with, I believe INTP (The Architect) is the closest representation of my Myers-Briggs personality test. People of this type are preoccupied with thoughts on how things work, what their purpose is, the structure of systems and tradition and how things could be improved. I resonate strongly with these thoughts.

The Big Five personality test results shows that I have a tendency towards abstract thinking, introversion and neuroticism. The results for the “Conscientiousness” category shows a lack of self-discipline. The “Agreeableness” category shows little preference for putting my needs above others or vice versa.

The Learning Style test results concluded that I am primarily an Auditory learner, as opposed to a Visual or Tactile learner. This means that, for example, if I were to be read a story by a teacher, I would learn it easier than if I had to either read it to myself in my head, view visual depictions of the story or act out the story (by myself or as a group).

How do you think these results may influence your behaviour in a team?

All of the results can be seen to likely influence my behaviour in a team. I will present some examples of how the results may influence my behaviour in a team, as well as strategies to work around problematic traits and maximise use of useful traits below:

INTROVERSION-As I am predominantly introverted, I may not assert myself enough, which affects the whole teamwork process (quantity and quality of my participation). I may struggle to voice my opinions and participate in team activities. I must make an effort to assert myself so that I can freely share my ideas, confidently participate in teamwork and provide constructive feedback to others in the team.

LOGICAL THINKING-I have a strong tendency toward logic and reason over emotions. It would likely mean that my judgement would be clearer of emotional influence, leading to more rational decisions. On the other hand, this logical dominance may not take into account others emotional needs enough in discussions, tasks and decisions when necessary. I must consciously observe and consider others emotions with more frequency when participating in the team.

LEARNING STYLES-Being that my primary learning style is auditory, incorporating audio-based components for team communication and participation would positively influence my behaviour in a team. All members of the team could also take into account each of our preferred learning styles when working together for increased team behaviour.

ABSTRACT/ANALYSIS/SCEPTIC-Because I am very analytical, value scepticism and often think in abstract, the thoughts I have that relate to my teams focus may draw attention to something useful that no one else would have thought about. I should focus the majority of my contribution to areas in the teamwork that benefit most from these thought patterns.

NEUROTICISM-My high levels of neuroticism could affect my behaviour in a team. The negative emotions that arise from team problems like under-performance, falling behind in work output, personal emotional strain between team members or bad teamwork could affect my behaviour in the team. My mood and work effort may plummet as a result. To minimise the number of instances and severity of situations that may lower my mood and work effort, the whole team must keep the quality of organisation, planning, communication and work output satisfactory.

How should you take this into account when forming a team?

For the best way to decide who I should make a team with, I should prioritise people who excel in traits that make up for a trait I lack, or if traits they have compliment any of my strong traits. For instance, someone with strong extroversion skills may improve communication and interpersonal relations both inside and outside the group. As I am an introvert, this is something I would likely find difficult to do myself. Also, finding other logical thinkers would compliment my lack of focus on emotions. There would be less instances of emotional conflict, and so sharing a preference for logic may also speed up the thought process due to increased exchange of ideas because of clearer minds.
